Sleep is essential for cognitive functioning (@smith2023). As demonstrated by @jones2024, even partial sleep deprivation measurably impairs decision-making ability. Multiple studies have replicated these findings [@smith2023; @brown2022].

# Method

## Participants

A total of 120 undergraduate students were recruited from the university participant pool. Screening criteria followed @williams2021[Table 2]. All participants reported normal sleep patterns prior to the study.

## Procedure

Participants were randomly assigned to either a sleep-deprived condition (24 hours awake) or a control condition (normal sleep). Cognitive assessments were administered the following morning, measuring attention, working memory, and decision-making.

> This study employed a between-subjects design to minimize carryover effects from sleep deprivation, ensuring that cognitive performance measures were independent across conditions.

# Results

Performance differed significantly between groups [@smith2023; @brown2022]. The sleep-deprived group showed a 35% reduction in attention scores and a 28% decline in working memory capacity.

# Discussion

These findings align with prior work on sleep and cognition (@smith2023; @jones2024). The observed impairments in attention and working memory are consistent with the guidelines outlined by @apa2020.
